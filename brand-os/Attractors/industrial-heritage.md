## Industrial Heritage

The structure always told the truth. Industrial heritage at scale — the brand inhabits and reinterprets existing industrial forms, turning former factories, power stations, and mills into destinations, residences, or cultural institutions. The aesthetic is material honesty: exposed brick, raw steel, original machinery as ornament. History is not erased — it is inhabited.

## Visual code
- **Typography:** Condensed or monospaced typography with industrial character — Neue Haas Grotesk Bold condensed, Courier Prime, or bespoke industrial lettering referencing original signage. Archival document aesthetic acceptable. High contrast between type and surface.
- **Palette:** Deep rust/terracotta, raw black, aged cream, exposed brick red. Palette drawn from the building's own materiality — no invented corporate colours. Archival photograph tones (sepia, silver gelatin) as secondary palette.
- **Geometry:** Angular and structural — the building's geometry is the brand geometry. Exposed beams, arched windows, steel rivets, chimney proportions. Artifact-as-ornament approach: original industrial elements become decorative heroes.
- **Density:** Medium — enough archival material and structural detail to feel rich. High-contrast editorial layouts with generous white space. The document aesthetic of found archives.
- **Textures:** Exposed brick at macro scale, patinated steel, original timber beams, industrial glass, concrete with aggregate. Every surface with visible material history. Nothing that looks new or invented.

## Verbal code
- **Tone:** Respectful of history without nostalgia or sentimentality. Material honesty as value. Monumental scale presented as natural fact, not boast. Speaks with the quiet authority of a place that has outlasted many purposes.
- **Phrase length:** Short declarative statements mixed with historical precision — year of construction, original function, names of architects. Facts as poetry.
- **Vocabulary:** Material precision (brick, steel, cast iron, turbine, chimney, rivet), temporal anchoring (1933, since, original, century, grade-listed), scale vocabulary (hall, chimney, nave, warehouse, span), transformation language (reinvented, inhabited, reimagined, alive again).
- **Examples:** "The structure always told the truth." / "Four chimneys. One icon. A hundred futures." / "History doesn't have to end — it can be inhabited."

## Industry adaptations

### Real estate
Loft residential conversion where original industrial elements — exposed brick, steel columns, original windows — are documented, preserved, and featured in marketing. Developer publishes archival photographs and construction history. Unit design celebrates the industrial skeleton, not conceals it.

### HoReCa
Hotel or restaurant within a heritage industrial building. Original machinery on display as art. Exposed services as aesthetic. Menu and programming reference the building's original function (coal, textiles, brewing). Event spaces in turbine halls, loading bays, and warehouses.

### Fashion
Brand whose identity borrows from workwear and industrial craft — denim, canvas, brass hardware, work boot sole. Campaign imagery shot in authentic industrial locations, not studio. Provenance of materials traced with precision. Repair and longevity as core values.

### Tech
Cultural tech platform or creative space operating from industrial heritage building. Brand identity borrows the building's character — heavy typography, documentary photography, material metaphors in UI. Community and maker culture as organizing principle.

## When to apply
- Project is a genuine redevelopment of an industrial heritage building (not imitation)
- Brand wants to own monumental scale, material authenticity, and historical depth simultaneously
- Audience values provenance and recognizes authenticity — cannot be faked by cosmetic brick wallpaper
- Brand needs to justify premium pricing through history and material honesty, not novelty
