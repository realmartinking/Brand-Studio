## American Airlines

Founded 1926, headquartered in Fort Worth, Texas. America's largest airline by fleet size and revenue passenger miles. The 2013 FutureBrand rebrand replaced the 1968 Massimo Vignelli identity (red, white, blue eagle with silver fuselage) with a new eagle mark and "flight symbol" — a stylized "AA" with motion lines suggesting wing movement. Member of the oneworld alliance. "Going for great" was a campaign positioning in the rebrand era.

## Why this position

Control-release 0.7: Aviation is a high-control operation — safety systems, schedules, air traffic control, crew management. The brand communicates reliability and operational mastery.

Distance 0.6: The airline-passenger relationship maintains formal distance. Unlike Airbnb, you don't know your pilot personally. The brand is experienced at scale.

All-few 0.3: Commercial aviation is mass transportation. American Airlines serves 200M+ passengers annually.

Nature-technology 0.6: Aviation is pure engineering — the technology of flight. The eagle logo bridges nature (the animal) and technology (the achievement of flight).

Repeatability 0.4: Business travelers fly frequently — American's AAdvantage loyalty program is built for repeat purchase. But leisure travelers are more episodic.

## Attractor difference

Corporate-trust fits: operational authority, American institutional presence, mass reliability. Key differences: the 2013 FutureBrand rebrand moved American significantly toward motion and aspiration — the new flight symbol is more dynamic than the old Vignelli eagle. This makes it slightly more [[silicon-optimism]]-adjacent in design language (clean, forward-moving, confident). The eagle heritage connects to [[warm-heritage]] in American iconography. American Airlines is less emotionally warm than Southwest, less premium than Delta's recent positioning — it occupies the solid institutional center of American aviation.
