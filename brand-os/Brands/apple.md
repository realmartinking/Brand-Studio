## Apple

The defining consumer technology brand of the past 30 years. Apple's position is unusual: it achieves mass scale while maintaining premium perception. The product IS the brand — the unboxing is a ritual, the software is the environment, the store is the temple.

## Why this position

Self-community 0.4: Apple products are individual (your phone, your computer) but embedded in a community — iMessage lock-in, AirDrop culture, the shared visual language of iOS. The community is invisible infrastructure.

Control-release 0.8: Total vertical integration — hardware, software, retail, service. Every interaction is designed. Nothing left to chance.

Roots-horizon 0.6: "Think Different" heritage (roots) plus M-chip and Vision Pro innovation (horizon). Apple manages both simultaneously — rare for a tech company.

All-few 0.4: Aspires to be for everyone but priced above the median. The result: aspirational mass market — enough people own Apple products for cultural ubiquity, but the price still signals something.

Nature-technology 0.8: Apple uses nature metaphors (Retina, Safari, macOS Sonoma landscapes) but is fundamentally a technology system brand. The warmth is designed — the technology is real.

Density 0.9 / Weight 0.8: Maximum whitespace in all marketing. The product floats. Nothing competes with it in the frame.

Rhythm 0.7 / Repeatability 0.2: Daily use (routine), but the annual keynote is a cultural event — the rhythm of excitement overlaid on the rhythm of habit.

Speed 0.6: The unboxing experience is deliberately ceremonial — multi-layer packaging, the sound of the seal, the smell of new device. Purchase as ritual.

## Attractor difference

Clean-tech attractor sits at all_few=0.5, roots_horizon=0.7, temperature=0.7 (cooler). Apple is slightly more accessible (0.4), slightly warmer (0.6) — the human element in Apple's communication (the "Shot on iPhone" campaigns, accessibility features) keeps it from being purely cold. Also more rooted: Apple carries 40 years of visual and cultural memory that clean-tech (as a position) typically lacks.

## Visual code
- **Typography:** San Francisco (proprietary) — a grotesque designed for screen legibility at all sizes. New York serif for editorial contexts. Precise spacing, consistent weight hierarchy.
- **Palette:** White, silver, space grey, midnight, starlight — all named like weather events or materials. Product color is the accent; brand system stays neutral.
- **Geometry:** Rounded rectangles everywhere — the icon grid, the device corners, the store fixtures. Soft precision, not hard minimalism.
- **Density:** Maximum whitespace — the product floats in the frame. One product per page. Marketing is anti-information, pro-sensation.
- **Textures:** Aluminum, glass, silicone — all machined to tolerance. The unboxing sequence: matte white cardboard, friction-fit lid, product in tissue. Touch is designed.

## Verbal code
- **Tone:** Confident, warm, non-technical despite technical claims. Speaks to aspiration, not specification.
- **Phrase length:** Very short declarative sentences. "This is iPhone." "Shot on iPhone." The period is the tone.
- **Vocabulary:** Emotional adjectives (magical, beautiful, powerful), capability as identity ("You've never seen anything like this"), human-centered framing (people, lives, moments).
- **Examples:** "The most personal device we've ever created." / "Shot on iPhone." / "Think Different."
