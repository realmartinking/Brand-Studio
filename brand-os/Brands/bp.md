## BP

British multinational oil and gas company, founded 1909 as Anglo-Persian Oil Company, headquartered in London. One of the world's seven major oil companies. The 2000 Landor rebrand — one of the most discussed in corporate history — replaced the BP shield logo with the "Helios" mark: a sunflower-like starburst in green and yellow. The accompanying "Beyond Petroleum" positioning attempted to reframe BP as an energy company moving beyond fossil fuels. The rebrand cost an estimated $211 million. Widely praised as brand strategy innovation at launch, widely criticised after the 2010 Deepwater Horizon disaster as greenwashing that made the gap between aspiration and reality more damaging. The "Beyond Petroleum" tagline was eventually retired. BP returned to "British Petroleum" heritage framing in some communications post-2010.

## Why this position

Roots-horizon 0.7: The "Beyond Petroleum" rebrand was explicitly a horizon move — positioning toward a renewable energy future. Post-Deepwater Horizon, the roots (oil heritage) reasserted themselves. The brand now holds both simultaneously: 100+ years of energy extraction plus stated transition commitments.

Nature-technology 0.4: The Helios logo uses sun/flower imagery — nature signifiers layered onto an industrial company. This is the core tension. BP is a technology-intensive extraction business presenting nature-coded visuals. The gap between code and reality is the brand's central contradiction.

All-few 0.2: BP operates at infrastructure scale — fuel for millions of vehicles daily, global supply chains. Mass exposure through forecourt retail.

Control-release 0.7: BP is a vertically integrated energy major — extraction, refining, distribution, retail. System control is the business model.

Participation 0.2: BP's forecourt presence is mass-market but transactional. No community, no engagement. The green/nature framing invites identification but cannot sustain it.

Temperature 0.4: The green/yellow palette reads as warm relative to fossil fuel industry norms, but the Deepwater Horizon legacy permanently cooled brand warmth. Cool-leaning despite the warm visual code.

Serious-playful 0.7: The Helios logo is one of the most optimistic marks ever created for a fossil fuel company. The seriousness of the business vs the lightness of the logo is an unresolved tension.

## Attractor difference

eco-monument is the nearest fit for the visual and aspirational identity of BP's post-2000 brand: green credentials, sun imagery, "Beyond Petroleum" as an environmental mission statement. The Helios logo shares visual DNA with eco-monument's nature-is-architecture philosophy. However, BP is a critical attractor mismatch case: the brand adopts eco-monument's visual and verbal codes without the substance those codes require. Key deviations: eco-monument brands (Bosco Verticale, 1 Hotel) deliver on their ecological claims — BP manifestly does not. This creates a permanent credibility discount that makes eco-monument an aspirational fit rather than an earned position. The more honest attractor is [[corporate-trust]] (what BP structurally is) with eco-monument as a communications aspiration that has repeatedly collapsed under scrutiny. Adjacent pull from [[warm-heritage]] for the original BP shield and oil heritage.

## Visual code

- **Typography:** Helvetica Neue — the house font for the post-2000 brand system. Clean, neutral, uncontroversially legible. The wordmark "bp" in lowercase with the Helios mark.
  *Source: training-data / Landor 2000 case*
- **Palette:** Sunflower Yellow (#FFDA00) and Viridian Green (#009900) — warm and cool together, suggesting sun and nature. White as background. The two-tone Helios is the visual centrepiece.
  *Source: training-data*
- **Geometry:** The Helios mark: a circular burst of pointed, tapered petals radiating from a centre, alternating in scale. Organic/natural geometry rather than corporate rectangles. The lowercase "bp" wordmark is round and approachable.
  *Source: training-data*
- **Density:** Forecourt signage is bold and functional. Brand advertising is typically aspirational — wide landscapes, low density, nature photography.
  *Source: training-data*
- **Textures:** In marketing: natural textures (light through leaves, ocean surface, sky). In reality: industrial infrastructure. The gap between texture and product is the brand's structural problem.
  *Source: training-data*

## Verbal code

- **Tone:** Aspirational, responsible, measured. Post-2010: more cautious and evidence-based, less visionary.
- **Phrase length:** Medium. Declarative claims with supporting data in corporate communications.
- **Vocabulary:** Energy transition language (low-carbon, net-zero, transition, future), scale markers (million barrels, global, infrastructure), responsibility framing (commitment, accountability, progress).
- **Examples:** "Beyond Petroleum." (retired) / "Keep advancing." / "Committed to net zero by 2050 or sooner."
