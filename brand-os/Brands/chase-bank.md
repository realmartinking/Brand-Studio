## Chase Bank

Chase Manhattan Bank, now the consumer banking division of JPMorgan Chase & Co. — America's largest bank by assets. The octagon logo was designed by Chermayeff & Geismar in 1960 for Chase Manhattan Bank — one of the earliest and most influential examples of modern corporate identity in American banking. The logo has remained essentially unchanged for 65 years. 4,700+ branches, 16,000+ ATMs. "Make more of what's yours" is the current consumer positioning.

## Why this position

Geometry 0.7: The octagon is a pure geometric form — no metaphor, no imagery, just shape. It communicates stability (solid form), completion (closed shape), and modernity (not a shield or column). One of design history's most confident gestures.

Control-release 0.7: Banking is the management and control of money — custody, systems, risk management. Trust is built through demonstrated control.

Repeatability 0.1: Banking is the ultimate daily habit. Chase card is swiped multiple times daily — maximum frequency touchpoint.

Weight 0.6: Banking products are weighty decisions — mortgages, investments, business accounts. The brand needs to feel substantial and reliable.

Serious-playful 0.6: Chase has moved toward more consumer-friendly, approachable positioning in recent years ("Make more of what's yours") without abandoning institutional seriousness.

## Attractor difference

Corporate-trust is the home: institutional authority, financial control, American establishment. Chase fits closely but with one significant departure: the octagon logo is so geometrically pure and modernist that it has design DNA more aligned with [[bauhaus-functional]] than traditional banking's heraldic aesthetics. The logo feels like it belongs to a tech company — it's more Nike than Goldman Sachs. This visual modernity was ahead of its time in 1960 and remains distinctive. Adjacent to [[friendly-helper]] for consumer banking accessibility and "Make more of what's yours" warmth.
