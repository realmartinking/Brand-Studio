## Kia

Kia Corporation was founded in 1944 in Seoul, South Korea and is part of the Hyundai Motor Group. For most of its international history Kia was positioned as a value-entry automotive brand; a strategic repositioning began with Peter Schreyer joining as Chief Design Officer (2006) and accelerated dramatically with the 2021 global rebrand. The new logo — a fluid, calligraphic wordmark often misread as "KN" — replaced the oval Kia badge, and the new brand platform "Movement that inspires" signals a decisive push toward premium, design-led positioning. The EV6, EV9, and EV3 electric vehicles have won multiple design awards (including World Car Design of the Year 2022 for EV6), validating the premium direction.

## Why this position

**roots_horizon 0.7:** The 2021 rebrand is an explicit horizon move — Kia is actively building a new identity rather than trading on heritage. The new logo has no continuity with the previous oval badge. The brand is betting on where it is going, not where it came from.

**nature_technology 0.6:** Kia's EV strategy places it in clean-technology territory; the EV line aesthetic (parametric surfaces, ambient interior lighting, sustainable materials) leans toward tech-nature synthesis. Not as cold as pure-tech brands, not as warm as heritage brands.

**all_few 0.4:** Post-rebrand Kia targets a more design-conscious segment than its value-brand history suggests. Still broadly accessible by automotive standards (C/D segment pricing), but aspiring upward. The EV9 three-row electric SUV at premium price points pushes toward 0.3; the Picanto at 0.6 pulls back.

**geometry 0.4:** The new logo's fluid calligraphic geometry (angular but flowing) and the EV body language (creased surfaces, sharp character lines) position Kia firmly in angular precision, not rounded softness.

**density 0.6:** Kia's design language is intentionally complex — layered surfaces, multiple body creases, detailed lighting signatures. The interior environments of EV6/EV9 are information-rich. Not minimalist — deliberately present.

**repeatability 0.7:** Cars are durable, multi-year purchases, but Kia's service network and fleet/corporate business create high-frequency brand touchpoints through the ownership cycle.

## Attractor difference

## Visual code

- **Typography:** The 2021 corporate typeface is a custom geometric sans — angular, with pointed terminals that reference the logo's calligraphic strokes. Clean, modern, with slightly condensed proportions for automotive context.
- **Palette:** White and black as primary brand system. Kia uses a sophisticated mid-tone palette for vehicle colourways (Moonscape grey, Aurora Black Pearl, Glacier White Pearl). Accent use of lime green in EV marketing materials signals electrification.
- **Geometry:** The new logo is angular calligraphy — fluid diagonals, no closed letterforms. Vehicle design language uses sharp creases (the "tiger nose" grille evolved into parametric surface patterns), not curves.
- **Density:** Medium-high — Kia's marketing communicates the design complexity of the vehicles. Multiple design angles, interior detail shots. Not sparse.
- **Textures:** Sustainable interior materials (recycled PET, bio-based paint options) as premium texture signal in EV models. Metallic exterior surfaces with complex paint finishes. Digital UI surfaces in cabin.

## Verbal code

- **Tone:** Aspirational, forward-looking, design-confident. The brand speaks about inspiration and movement rather than reliability and value — a deliberate departure from its previous positioning.
- **Phrase length:** Medium — headline-length statements with emotional resonance. Not corporate-formal, not casual.
- **Vocabulary:** Motion vocabulary (movement, momentum, inspire, drive, progress), design vocabulary (crafted, bold, unexpected), future-orientation (tomorrow, forward, electric).
- **Examples:** "Movement that inspires." / "Drive the change." / "Designed to be desired." / "Opposites United." (EV6 campaign)
