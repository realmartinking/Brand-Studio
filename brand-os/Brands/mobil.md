## Mobil

Mobil Corporation, founded as Standard Oil of New York (Socony) in 1866, rebranded to Mobil in 1966. Chermayeff & Geismar redesigned the identity — the red "o" in Mobil and the Pegasus flying horse logo became one of the most celebrated examples of corporate identity design of the 20th century. Merged with Exxon in 1999 to form ExxonMobil, one of the world's largest companies. The Mobil visual identity, particularly the Pegasus, remains in use at service stations globally despite the corporate merger.

## Why this position

Contrast 0.7: The red "o" against the blue-black Mobil wordmark is a deliberate, high-contrast design move — color as differentiator in a commodity industry. The Pegasus is red on white or blue backgrounds.

Nature-technology 0.7: Oil is the extraction and transformation of natural resource into technological fuel. The brand sits firmly in industrial technology, not nature.

Control-release 0.7: Energy infrastructure is about control — supply chains, refinery operations, pricing systems. The brand communicates reliability and mastery of complex systems.

Serious-playful 0.6: The Pegasus — a mythological flying horse — is an unusually playful choice for an oil company. It elevates the brand above pure utility into aspiration. This is the genius of the Chermayeff & Geismar work.

All-few 0.2: Fuel is a universal commodity. Mobil stations serve everyone with a vehicle.

## Attractor difference

Corporate-trust is the structural home: industrial authority, infrastructure control, serious operations. But Mobil deviates significantly: the Pegasus and red "o" give it genuine design wit that pure corporate-trust brands avoid. The identity is almost playful in its mythological reference — an oil company choosing a flying horse is a surprising, confident, and elegant move. This places Mobil adjacent to [[bauhaus-functional]] (clean industrial design logic) and [[silicon-optimism]] (bold reduction to a single memorable symbol). The red "o" is a proto-Apple move — color as brand differentiator in a sea of generic competitors.

## Verbal code

- **Tone:** Confident, industrial, reliable. The infrastructure brand that chooses mythology rather than corporate vocabulary.
- **Phrase length:** Short, declarative. Service brands don't need poetry.
- **Vocabulary:** Power, performance, engine, fuel, Pegasus, quality, protection, drive.
- **Examples:** "Mobil 1 — The World's Leading Synthetic Motor Oil." / "Mobil — Thinking of You."
