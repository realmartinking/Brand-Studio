## Nike

Nike, Inc. was founded in 1964 as Blue Ribbon Sports by Phil Knight and Bill Bowerman in Eugene, Oregon; it became Nike in 1971, named for the Greek goddess of victory. The Swoosh was designed by Carolyn Davidson in 1971 for $35. "Just Do It" was introduced in 1988. Nike is the world's largest supplier of athletic shoes, apparel, and equipment, operating across 170+ countries. Its business model centres on athlete endorsements (Michael Jordan in 1984 was the pivot moment), creating the Air Jordan sub-brand that transcended sport into culture. Nike's cultural power derives from the intersection of athletic performance, celebrity culture, streetwear, and political voice (Colin Kaepernick 2018 campaign).

## Why this position

**all_few 0.2:** Nike is for everyone who moves. The "Just Do It" philosophy extends from elite athletes to casual joggers to people who wear Nike without ever exercising. Mass global distribution across all price tiers. One of the most inclusive brand propositions in consumer goods.

**loud_quiet 0.1:** The Swoosh is one of the loudest marks in the world — recognisable at five metres on a shoe, on a billboard from 200 metres. Nike never whispers. The brand shouts through athlete bodies, cultural moments, and saturation-level marketing.

**speed 0.1:** Nike's fundamental value proposition is speed, acceleration, and performance. The brand moves fast — in drops, campaign response, cultural commentary. This is maximum speed in brand expression.

**body_mind 0.3:** Sportswear is fundamentally about the body — physical performance, sweat, movement, physicality. Nike's communication is relentlessly body-forward: athlete bodies in motion, training footage, race-day footage.

**self_community 0.3:** Nike's aspiration and "Just Do It" rhetoric is fundamentally about individual achievement — your personal best, your moment, your decision to start. Community (running clubs, NRC app) exists, but the core message is the individual athlete's will.

**rhythm 0.1:** Nike operates at cultural high speed — constant drops, athlete moments, campaign releases, collab announcements. The brand never stops moving.

## Visual code

- **Typography:** Nike uses Futura Bold condensed and custom display grotesque in advertising — heavy, impact-optimised type. The Nike wordmark is a clean sans. Product communications use ultra-bold weight to match the brand's high-energy register.
- **Palette:** White and black as primary system, with Nike's brand presence communicated through the Swoosh alone rather than a proprietary colour. Product lines carry their own colour stories (Jordan red/black, Air Max neon, Dri-FIT volt green). The brand itself is colour-agnostic — the Swoosh adapts to any surface.
- **Geometry:** The Swoosh — a single fluid stroke, asymmetric, kinetic. Implies forward motion, speed, a wing or a checkmark. Instantly recognisable at any scale, in any colour. Curved and angular simultaneously.
- **Density:** Low in premium campaign contexts — athlete, Swoosh, tagline. Higher density in product communications (technology callouts, materials, performance specs).
- **Textures:** Performance fabric textures (Flyknit mesh, Air unit foam, Dri-FIT weave) in product photography. Campaign imagery is clean, high-contrast, motion-blurred. No decorative textures.

## Verbal code

- **Tone:** Motivational, direct, slightly challenging. Nike believes you are capable of more than you think. The brand is on your side but will not let you make excuses.
- **Phrase length:** Extremely short — three words or fewer at its most iconic. "Just Do It" is three words. "Find Your Greatness." is three words. The imperative is the format.
- **Vocabulary:** Action imperatives (do, run, just, find, push, move), aspiration nouns (greatness, champion, winner, athlete), universal second person (you, your), athletic specificity when needed.
- **Examples:** "Just Do It." / "You can't stop us." / "Find your greatness." / "There is no finish line."
