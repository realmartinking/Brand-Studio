## Wii

The Nintendo Wii was launched in November 2006 as Nintendo's seventh-generation home console, designed under the leadership of Satoru Iwata and Shigeru Miyamoto as a deliberate departure from the power-specification arms race between Sony PlayStation 3 and Microsoft Xbox 360. The Wii's revolutionary motion-sensing Wii Remote controller made physical movement the core game mechanic, enabling non-gamers (children, elderly, non-gaming adults) to participate for the first time. Wii Sports, bundled with the console, became the best-selling game in history for many years. The tagline "Wii would like to play" and the name itself (phonetic "we") embedded community and togetherness into the brand identity. The console sold over 101 million units, outselling both competitors.

## Why this position

**participation 0.9:** Wii is the highest-participation gaming brand ever created. The controller demands physical movement — you do not press a button, you throw a bowling ball or swing a tennis racket. Non-gamers who had never held a controller participated. Nursing homes ran Wii bowling tournaments. This is maximum participation.

**all_few 0.1:** The entire Wii strategy was "for everyone" — explicitly including people who had never played games. Grandparents, toddlers, non-gamers at parties. The most democratically inclusive gaming platform in history.

**self_community 0.8:** "Wii" = "we." The name encodes community. Wii Sports is always multi-player or audience-participatory. The console is designed for the living room, not the bedroom. Social play is the core use case.

**roots_horizon 0.8:** The Wii was a radical innovation — nothing existed like the Wii Remote before. It created a new category (motion gaming) and inspired competitors (PlayStation Move, Kinect). Maximum horizon move.

**body_mind 0.3:** The Wii externalises game mechanics into physical movement — you use your body, not your thumbs. This is the most body-forward gaming brand. Tennis, boxing, golf, bowling — all physical activities rendered in digital play.

**distance 0.2:** The brand is maximally intimate and informal. "Wii would like to play" — playful, inviting, using first-person plural. The controller is called a Wiimote. The whole identity system is warm and accessible.

## Visual code

- **Typography:** The Wii logotype uses a rounded, friendly sans-serif — lowercase, white on a warm blue-grey or white background. Rounded letterforms echo the console's smooth, curved industrial design. Accessible and approachable at all sizes.
- **Palette:** White (#FFFFFF) as primary — the console itself is white, unusually for electronics. Sky blue and warm grey as secondary. Wii Sports uses primary palette (blue sky, green grass) within games. The system is warm-neutral, not cold-tech.
- **Geometry:** Rounded rectangles throughout — the console form, the Wiimote, the sensor bar, the Nunchuk. The Wii logo itself uses rounded letterforms. Nothing sharp or angular.
- **Density:** Low — white space dominates. The console's design is a white slab with a single blue LED. Marketing is clean and product-forward.
- **Textures:** Smooth piano-black gloss on the console disc slot; matte white body. Tactile rubber grip on Wiimote. No decorative textures — the product texture is functional.

## Verbal code

- **Tone:** Warm, inviting, slightly playful. Speaks to everyone simultaneously — the non-gamer and the gamer. No jargon. Motion as metaphor for participation.
- **Phrase length:** Short and conversational. "Wii would like to play" is both invitation and pun — simple enough for any age.
- **Vocabulary:** Inclusive pronouns (we, together, everyone, family), play vocabulary (fun, play, move, game, sport), physical action verbs (swing, throw, bowl, dance).
- **Examples:** "Wii would like to play." / "Play together." / "Get in the game." / "Everyone plays."
